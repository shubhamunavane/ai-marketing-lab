## The Setup

ChatGPT ads are no longer a hypothetical. They are live, priced, and attracting real brand budgets. But the signal from early participants tells a complicated story — and the details matter more than the headline.

Criteo — the programmatic infrastructure layer behind thousands of retargeting and commerce campaigns — is now OpenAI's first official programmatic partner. That integration puts roughly 17,000 advertisers within reach of ChatGPT's ad inventory, even if most of them are not actively aware of it.

Early brands running test placements include Williams-Sonoma and The Knot. Both operate in high-consideration categories where conversational context has real value — home goods buying decisions and wedding planning are categories where someone asking a question is already partway down the funnel. That context is the entire thesis of conversational advertising. It is a genuinely interesting thesis.

## What the Inventory Actually Looks Like

The mechanics are still emerging, but the broad shape is this: ads appear as part of, or alongside, ChatGPT responses. The user is in active research or decision-making mode — not scrolling passively through a feed.

The pricing reflects that positioning, at least on paper. CPMs are reportedly around $60. For context, Facebook feed CPMs average $8–$15 depending on audience and objective. Premium programmatic display typically runs $5–$15 CPM. ChatGPT is priced as a premium channel before it has the measurement infrastructure of one.

Minimum commitments are reported at approximately $200,000. That threshold immediately narrows the viable audience to mid-market and enterprise advertisers — this is not a self-serve channel for lean growth teams.

## Why Performance Marketers Should Pay Attention

The intent density inside a ChatGPT conversation is unlike anything else in the current media landscape.

When someone types "What's the best stand mixer for a home baker?" into ChatGPT, they are not browsing. They are actively trying to make a decision. That is closer to branded search than social — and branded search has historically delivered the best conversion economics in digital advertising.

If the ad format can match that intent coherently — if a Williams-Sonoma placement appears naturally when someone is asking about kitchen equipment — the click quality could be exceptional. For media buyers managing e-commerce or considered-purchase categories, this format has structural potential that generic display and broad social never had.

The question is not whether conversational advertising can work. The question is whether it works yet, for most budgets, at these prices.

## The Real Problems

**Measurement does not exist.** There is no standardized attribution model for ChatGPT ads. No pixel equivalent. No clear signal path from ad exposure to purchase. For performance marketing teams that make decisions based on CPL, CPA, and ROAS, running budget without measurement infrastructure is operationally very difficult to justify. You cannot optimize what you cannot measure.

**Brand safety is unresolved.** Programmatic advertising spent a decade building brand safety controls for social and open web inventory. Conversational AI responses are dynamic and context-dependent — the audit process is substantially more complex. The infrastructure to prevent a brand appearing in an unwanted context does not appear to be mature yet.

**No one is actually moving budget.** Industry commentary from early participants confirms what common sense suggests: this is incremental test spend, not a reallocation. Nobody is reducing spend on retail media networks or performance search to fund ChatGPT ads. When the people writing the checks describe it as a learning expense, that tells you where the channel actually sits in the adoption curve.

**The Google signal is worth reading carefully.** Google DeepMind leadership has publicly indicated that Gemini may not carry ads, framing advertising as a trust risk for AI assistants. Google's entire business depends on advertising — if they are calling out the trust risk in AI ads publicly, they are either genuinely concerned or positioning for a different approach. Either way, the dominant player in search advertising is not rushing in.

## Actionable Takeaways

- **Don't shift performance budget.** Until measurement infrastructure exists, you have no way to evaluate ROI with any rigor. Spending without measurement is brand activity, not performance marketing.
- **Track the case studies.** Williams-Sonoma and The Knot are running the pilot on behalf of the industry. Watch for any published CPA or CPL benchmarks. That data will be worth more than any speculation available now.
- **Qualify your category fit first.** High-consideration verticals with strong conversational intent — home, travel, financial products, health, professional services — are the natural fit. If your product is not something someone would actively ask a question about, the format is structurally weaker.
- **Define your measurement requirements before you spend.** If a $200K test is accessible for your budget, know exactly what signals you need to see to continue, scale, or cut the investment.
- **Watch Criteo's roadmap.** As the programmatic gateway, Criteo will likely build reporting and brand safety tooling faster than OpenAI will. Their infrastructure updates will signal when the channel is operationally ready for performance buyers.

## The Longer Arc

Conversational AI is going to be a significant media surface. The question is not whether advertising will exist inside these platforms — it clearly will. The question is when the format, measurement, and pricing will align well enough to deliver reliable, auditable returns.

That point is probably 12 to 24 months away for most performance marketing teams. The brands spending $200K now are buying early access to that future — not necessarily buying efficient conversions today. That is a legitimate strategy if you have the budget and the patience for ambiguity.

It is not a replacement for running efficient campaigns on proven channels.
